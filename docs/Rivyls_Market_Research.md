## Section 1 — What People Say About Fantasy Sports

*The following research compiles what average players — and non-players — say about fantasy football based on reviews, forum discussions, editorial commentary, and published research.*

---

### What People Love About Fantasy Football

**Social connection is the #1 driver.**
This comes up constantly and across all demographics. Research published in *Addictive Behaviors Reports* identified fantasy football's most significant benefits as creating friendships, maintaining relationships, and fostering a sense of community. People describe leagues that have run for 15–30 years as the primary reason old friend groups stay connected after moving apart, having kids, and going separate ways. One longtime player described his league as "the one thing that keeps us connected, even though we all moved away, had families, and stopped seeing each other."

**Skin in every game.**
Fantasy football gives players a vested interest in games they'd otherwise ignore — players describe watching matchups they would have skipped entirely because they have players competing in them.

**The GM/ownership fantasy.**
People love the feeling of being in control — drafting, managing trades, making lineup decisions, outsmarting others. Fantasy football scratches a competitive itch for people who grew up playing sports and still crave that outlet without the physical risk.

**Learning the sport.**
Within a month of playing, casual fans say they go from knowing only star players to knowing every team, every injury, and emerging players league-wide — simply as a byproduct of following the game more closely.

**Bragging rights and trash talk.**
The social theater around the draft, the group chats, the weekly banter — people describe this as often more enjoyable than the actual game results.

---

### What People Don't Like (Even If They Play)

**Time commitment.**
This is the most universal complaint. One former player described the commitment as "daily, for months on end," saying missing a single day felt like "fantasy suicide" — and that even in no-stakes environments, the time demands created resentment from family.

**Too much luck.**
A common frustration is that getting to the playoffs feels like skill, but winning in the playoffs feels like luck — one injury in the wrong week can torpedo a dominant team. Some quit entirely because they don't want to compete in something with too high a luck factor.

**Divided team loyalty.**
Having fantasy players on opposing teams creates a described "moral crisis" — fans find themselves emotionally whiplashed every few minutes, celebrating touchdowns for their fantasy team while their real team gives up points. Multiple people say this is what ruined the pure experience of fandom for them.

**Gambling adjacency and money pressure.**
In daily fantasy formats, 91% of winnings go to just 1.3% of players — largely algorithmic "superplayers" — but most casual participants don't realize how stacked the odds are against them. Many people who used to play stepped away when the gambling element grew more prominent.

**Stress and emotional toll.**
Experts note that when fantasy sports become seriously competitive and begin competing with work or affecting relationships, what started as entertainment becomes a source of anxiety, frustration, and mood swings.

---

### Why People Say They Don't Start at All

**"I don't know enough."**
The most common reason non-players give is simply not knowing the game well enough — feeling like they'd need to study extensively just to be competitive, and not wanting to commit to losing badly against people who've been doing it for years.

**"It takes too much time."**
This pairs with the above. The perception (often accurate) is that doing it right requires daily monitoring of stats, injuries, waiver wires, and matchups. Casual interest isn't enough.

**"I don't want to pay to lose."**
Non-players specifically cite not wanting to pay money into a league knowing they'll likely get demolished by more experienced players.

**"I actually like football."**
A surprisingly common stated reason — that tracking individual player stats pulls attention away from team strategy, coaching decisions, and the actual sport they love. The argument is that fantasy football reduces complex human athletic performance to math, which strips away the passion and storytelling of real fandom.

**"It'll cause drama."**
Several people mention not wanting the social friction that inevitably comes with money on the line — noting that the number of people who've gotten into real, lasting fights over fantasy sports is "astronomically high."

---

### What This Means for Rivyls

**The divided loyalty problem is completely solved by the program-drafting model.**
When you draft programs/schools instead of players, you can still root wholeheartedly for your team. If Alabama is your team, drafting Alabama doesn't pit you against yourself. That's a real, genuinely differentiating answer to one of the top stated reasons people resent traditional fantasy.

**The knowledge barrier is lower with programs than individual players.**
Most college football fans already have strong opinions about programs — rankings, conferences, traditional powerhouses vs. rising challengers. You don't need to know a running back's snap count history to have a confident opinion about Ohio State vs. Vanderbilt.

**The time commitment concern is worth keeping front of mind for UX design.**
Anything that reduces the weekly management burden (simple lineup decisions, minimal roster moves) could be a meaningful selling point for the people who liked the idea of fantasy but felt burned by the grind.

## Section 2 — Competitor Research: Traditional Platforms

*The major players break into two tiers: the mass-market platforms (ESPN, Yahoo, NFL.com) and the power-user platforms (Sleeper, Fantrax, Underdog). There is also a college-football-specific layer examined separately at the end of this section.*

---

### ESPN Fantasy Football

**What they do well:**
- Largest user base — ESPN serves more than 12 million users across all devices. That network effect is real. People use ESPN because everyone else already does.
- Weekly report cards, TD celebrations, and recap features that add personality and entertainment to the experience.
- The ESPN Fantasy App has a draft board and "draft train" that provides players with more information than ever during their drafts.
- Free to use — owned by Disney and has resisted monetizing beyond advertising.

**What they do poorly:**
- During Week 2 of the 2025 NFL season, ESPN's fantasy football app had major technical issues causing serious frustration for millions of fans. Their infrastructure buckles under peak load — a recurring problem.
- Users on social media called the app "horrendous," with a "garbage layout" that is "unbearably slow" and "as inconvenient and unintuitive as it gets."
- Flooded with advertisements and ESPN editorial content, which many users find cluttered and distracting.
- Very limited customization — works for casual leagues but falls apart for serious commissioners.

**Rivyls opportunity:** ESPN has 12M users who are largely captive through inertia, not love. The product hasn't meaningfully innovated and users are vocal about how bad the experience is. This is a dissatisfied base that can be poached.

---

### Yahoo Fantasy

**What they do well:**
- Yahoo fantasy football has more than 10 million registered users.
- Yahoo's standout feature is its rich historical data — users can look back at years of league records, head-to-head history, and performance trends, making it the most nostalgic platform for friend groups that have played together for a decade or more.
- AI-powered weekly recaps add personality and narrative to league results, turning dry stats into entertaining stories.
- One of the only platforms that combines season-long leagues and DFS in the same app.

**What they do poorly:**
- The biggest complaint: the messaging system was gutted. Users say chat is now so watered-down that nobody uses it, and their whole leagues moved to Facebook Messenger or group texts instead. This is a massive failure because social interaction is the #1 reason people play.
- Yahoo is extremely slow to react and often times out — entire leagues report this issue across devices.
- Scoring glitches where points don't correctly reflect real-life stats have cost people games.
- ESPN and Yahoo have both stopped innovating and feel like they're coasting on legacy while newer apps eat their most engaged users.

**Rivyls opportunity:** Yahoo's social features — their biggest differentiator — are broken and users are screaming about it. Chat and social engagement are pillars Rivyls has already built. That's a direct answer to Yahoo's biggest failure.

---

### Sleeper

**What they do well:**
- Sleeper's branding and design is sleek and genuinely cool, with live-tracking available for all sports and a dynamic multiplier system for customizing contests.
- Best-in-class social layer. League chat with read receipts, push notifications for trade proposals, squad features. Chat actually works and people use it.
- Strong mobile-first design. Very popular with younger audiences.
- Supports redraft, dynasty, keeper, best ball, and pick'em in a single app.

**What they do poorly:**
- News and injury updates are consistently late, even well after other sources have broken them. Game tracking often lags, and there's no good way to follow a game live.
- The interface, while visually appealing, is confusing in practice — multiple hidden menus, no indicators for basic actions like add/drop, and small tap targets on mobile.
- No obvious way to find and join public leagues. There's no sortable public league list, which means new users have no entry point if they don't already have a group of friends to play with.
- Finding leagues requires posting on forums and constantly bumping posts to stay visible.

**Rivyls opportunity:** Sleeper solved the social problem but created a discoverability problem. New users with no existing group have nowhere to go. A public league browser with filtering is a gap Rivyls can own. Their data quality issues (late news, inaccurate projections) are also a direct opening.

---

### Fantrax

**What they do well:**
- Fantrax boasts a unique database of collegiate players, making it the go-to choice for college football fantasy leagues. It is currently the primary platform serious college fantasy players use.
- The free version of Fantrax will satisfy just about any manager — clean, modern-looking interface with intuitive functionality.
- Endless customization. Scoring categories, roster formats, draft types, league sizes — everything is configurable.
- Exceptional customer support. Multiple reviewers specifically named individual support reps and praised response times — unusual in this industry.

**What they do poorly:**
- Users repeatedly say the depth of customization makes it "not user friendly" — it's "basically a desktop site for an app that has way too much going on."
- For college football specifically, Fantrax doesn't offer updated weekly player rankings or projections, which users say is a significant gap compared to the NFL product.
- Users who play college leagues on Fantrax describe being "rather indifferent" — they use it because there are no better options, not because they love it.
- Mobile experience is noticeably weaker than desktop, which is a problem for a sport that people follow on their phones.
- Notifications are broken — deleted notifications reappear, and there's no bulk-delete function.

**Rivyls opportunity:** Fantrax is the closest thing to a direct competitor in the college football space — and its users openly say they use it by default, not by preference. The college product is clearly under-invested compared to their NFL offering. This is Rivyls' clearest lane.

---

### Underdog Fantasy

**What they do well:**
- Underdog has set the standard for Best Ball drafts — draft once, no lineup management, no waivers, no trades. The highest-scoring lineup is set automatically each week. This directly solves the time-commitment complaint.
- Lightning-fast mobile experience, very simple to use.
- Strong community and word of mouth growth.

**What they do poorly:**
- Best Ball means very low engagement after the draft. There's no real in-season management, so the social glue is thin.
- Very NFL-focused. College is not a priority.
- Pick'em format creeps toward gambling territory and is banned in several states.

**Rivyls opportunity:** Underdog proved there's a massive market for low-maintenance formats. A "draft programs and let it ride" mode for Rivyls could capture users who love the draft experience but hate the weekly management grind.

---

### The College Football Gap — Where No One Is Playing

None of the major platforms have built a dedicated, polished college football experience. College fantasy football is described as a "rapidly increasing hobby" but still largely operates through workarounds — using Fantrax's NFL infrastructure with college players bolted on.

**Specific gaps no one has filled:**

**1. School/program-based drafting.**
Every platform is built around individual players. No one has built a product where you draft programs. This is Rivyls' core differentiator and there is literally no competition.

**2. Casual-friendly college format.**
Starting a college fantasy league for the first time introduces significant obstacles — player pools, scoring nuances, and conference structures that are unfamiliar to people used to the NFL format. Nobody has simplified this for the casual college fan.

**3. Real loyalty alignment.**
Traditional fantasy forces divided team loyalty. With programs, a Georgia fan drafts Georgia. The loyalty conflict that is the #1 emotional complaint about traditional fantasy simply doesn't exist.

**4. Social/discovery layer for college fans.**
College football fandom is intensely regional and tribal. There's no platform building community tools around conference rivalries, alma mater pride, or school-based smack talk — which is the most natural social layer for this audience.

**5. Projected weekly rankings for college.**
Even Fantrax's most loyal college users say the lack of updated weekly college rankings is the biggest gap in the platform. For a program-based game this is less complex than individual player projections, which actually gives Rivyls an advantage.

## Section 3 — Competitor Research: Betting-Adjacent Platforms

*PrizePicks, DraftKings, and FanDuel are not competitors to Rivyls in the traditional sense — they are gambling products, and Rivyls is a skill-based fantasy sports platform. But they are important to understand because they define what millions of sports fans have been trained to expect from a sports engagement app — and what is burning those same users out.*

---

### PrizePicks

**What they are:** The largest independent DFS brand in the U.S. by market share. A pick'em format — you select 2–5 players and predict whether each will go over or under a stat projection. No lineup building, no salary cap, no opponent drafting. The format eliminates the complexity of traditional rosters and salary caps, making it approachable for novice players.

**What they do well:**
- Simplest onboarding in the space. The app is praised for its clean design, easy navigation, and quick load times — pick 2–5 players, tap more/less, submit. You can go from download to first entry in under two minutes.
- Power Play / Flex Play system. Users can choose their risk level — all picks must hit for a higher payout, or partial hits still pay out at lower amounts. This risk-adjustment layer is genuinely clever.
- Accessibility. Users who gave up on DraftKings and FanDuel because building full lineups was too difficult found PrizePicks easy enough to get into immediately.
- College sports included. They do offer college football and basketball props, which neither DraftKings nor FanDuel does robustly.
- Massive reach. PrizePicks now operates legally in 45 states and Washington D.C.

**What they do poorly:**
- Trust and money handling is catastrophically bad. PrizePicks has a 1.7-star rating on PissedConsumer, with the most common complaints being withheld funds, failed withdrawals, unauthorized transactions, account bans without explanation, and repeated customer service failures.
- Account closures when users win. Multiple reviewers across platforms describe a pattern: accounts are closed without reason when users become profitable, funds are held, and the only resolution comes after filing a BBB complaint.
- Customer service is chatbot-first and nearly useless. Withdrawal problems, location verification glitches, and app crashes are met with AI responses and chat abandonment.
- Legal exposure. In January 2024, New York and six other states rejected PrizePicks' pick'em format, classifying it as sports betting rather than DFS because players compete directly against the house. They had to restructure to a peer-to-peer format in 2025 to regain access to those markets.

**The deeper problem:** PrizePicks is essentially a gambling product dressed in fantasy sports clothing. The house sets the lines, the house profits. Platforms like PrizePicks quietly reduce entry limits for winning players, and users are noticing. The "shadow limiting" pattern — welcoming losers and restricting winners — is eroding trust across the whole pick'em DFS segment.

---

### DraftKings

**What they are:** One of the two giants of American sports betting and daily fantasy sports. Started as a DFS platform, now primarily a sportsbook operating in most legal betting states. Also the exclusive official sportsbook and odds provider of ESPN following a partnership announced in November 2025.

**What they do well:**
- Best app in the sportsbook space, technically. DraftKings maintains a 4.8/5 iOS rating and 4.6/5 Android rating from over 130,000 reviews.
- Live betting is exceptional. 54% of DraftKings' 2025 sports betting handle came from live bets. The platform averages 517 live betting options per game.
- Breadth of product. DFS, sportsbook, casino, pick'em, and now prediction markets — all under one account.
- Promotions. New user offers, parlay boosts, and ongoing promos are well-structured and don't require impossible play-through terms.
- Financial scale. DraftKings reported 43% year-over-year revenue growth in Q4 2025, ending the fiscal year with $6.05 billion in revenue.

**What they do poorly:**
- Customer service is widely despised. DraftKings has a 1.8-star rating from over 850 reviews on PissedConsumer, with only 14% of users likely to recommend the platform. The most frequent complaint: no phone support, chatbot-only responses, and ignored emails.
- Withdrawal problems when users win big. Users report accounts being limited or frozen when they go on winning streaks — bets are accepted during losses but withdrawal requests trigger verification delays when users try to cash out.
- The app pushes monetization aggressively. One reviewer said the first screen upon login was a "make a deposit" prompt, with money leaving their bank "faster than a 56k dial-up handshake."
- Regulatory and legal troubles. DraftKings paid a $425,000 settlement to Ohio for allowing prop bets on individual college players, paid $3 million in refunds to Connecticut customers over unclear bonus terms, and faces a Massachusetts class action over deposit bonus disclosure.
- Overwhelming for casual users. The sheer volume of options creates a cluttered interface that intimidates new users.

---

### FanDuel

**What they are:** America's largest sportsbook by total handle, originally a DFS company. Now owned by Flutter Entertainment (the global betting giant behind Betfair and Paddy Power). FanDuel pioneered the same-game parlay feature and was the first betting app to offer live streaming of sports events in 2019.

**What they do well:**
- Best live betting interface in the industry. The live betting section is visually appealing, intuitive, and offers a rich variety of in-game bets — 100+ options per game is common, with visual cues showing when and how odds shift.
- Polished, fast app. The FanDuel homepage loads in 1.7 seconds on desktop and 2.1 seconds on mobile — bet placement averages 3–5 seconds from selection to confirmation.
- Market depth and variety. FanDuel covers more niche markets and creative futures than most competitors — team specials, starting QB bets, player award futures, and more.
- Responsible gaming tools. They offer deposit limits, timeout periods, and self-exclusion — more prominent than most competitors.

**What they do poorly:**
- Withdrawal delays and account lockouts. The most consistent negative theme across thousands of reviews is withdrawal requests getting "stuck," accounts being suspended without explanation, and funds being held for days or weeks after approval emails are sent.
- Customer service is nearly non-existent. No phone support. Chat is slow. Users describe it as "virtually impossible" to get legitimate help.
- App performance degrades during peak moments. Live bet and cash-out options went down during early NFL games on multiple reported Sundays — FanDuel acknowledged the outage but declined to compensate affected users.
- Overwhelming for new users. The large menu of betting options can be overstimulating for people new to sports betting.

### The Pattern Across All Three — And What It Means for Rivyls

**The trust crisis is industry-wide.**
PrizePicks, DraftKings, and FanDuel all share the same pattern: easy to deposit, nightmare to withdraw, accounts restricted when users win. Users are noticing "shadow limiting" across all three platforms, and trust is eroding rapidly across the DFS/betting segment. Rivyls operates in an entirely different legal and ethical category — no house edge, no prop lines, no account restrictions for winning — and that difference is genuinely compelling in the current environment.

**The gambling adjacency problem is growing.**
Seven states rejected PrizePicks' original format as sports betting rather than DFS, and DraftKings is pushing into prediction markets despite warnings from state regulators. The regulatory tide is tightening. Rivyls' positioning as a NAICS 713990 skill-based entertainment product — with no house, no money lines, and peer-to-peer competition — is a meaningful strategic advantage as scrutiny on these platforms grows.

**Simplicity wins new users.**
The single biggest lesson PrizePicks taught the market is that lowering the barrier to entry dramatically expands the addressable audience. Their "pick 2 players, hit submit" format pulled in people who gave up on DraftKings salary cap DFS. Rivyls' "draft programs you already care about" model is the same instinct applied to college football — and it goes one step further by eliminating divided loyalty entirely.

**Customer service is the most differentiable soft feature in the space.**
Every single major platform — ESPN, Yahoo, DraftKings, FanDuel, PrizePicks — gets destroyed in customer service reviews. DraftKings' most common complaint summary is: "No customer service, no support number, you can't talk to someone." For a startup like Rivyls that can actually respond to users personally, this is an opening that costs nothing to exploit.
